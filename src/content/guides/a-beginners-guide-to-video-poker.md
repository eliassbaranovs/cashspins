# A Beginner's Guide to Video Poker

If you are looking for a casino game that combines the excitement of slots with the strategic depth of poker, learning how to play video poker is your next step. This guide will walk you through the mechanics of the game, explain how to identify the best machines, and provide you with foundational strategies to improve your odds. By the end, you will understand why video poker offers some of the best return-to-player (RTP) percentages in the casino and how to approach your first hand with confidence.

## What is Video Poker? The Slot-Poker Hybrid

Video poker is a unique casino game that blends the simplicity of a slot machine with the skill-based decision-making of five-card draw poker. Unlike traditional slot machines, where outcomes are purely random and determined by a **Random Number Generator (RNG)**, video poker allows your choices to directly influence the game's outcome. This means you are not just hoping for luck; you are actively playing to build the best possible poker hand.

The game is played on a console, either a physical machine in a land-based casino or a digital interface at an online casino. You place a bet, receive five cards, choose which cards to hold, and then draw new cards to replace the discards. Your final five-card hand is then evaluated against a **paytable**, which displays the payouts for different poker hands. This blend of chance and strategy is what makes video poker so appealing to players seeking better odds and more engagement than typical slots. In fact, many players consider video poker a superior option to slots due to its transparency and the player's ability to influence the outcome.

## Step-by-Step: How to Play Your First Hand

Playing video poker is straightforward once you understand the basic flow. Here is a step-by-step breakdown of a typical round:

1.  **Insert Money or Deposit Funds:**
 *   **Land-based casino:** Insert cash into the bill acceptor on the machine. The cash is converted into credits, typically displayed at the bottom of the screen.
 *   **Online casino:** Ensure your account is funded. You will see your balance displayed, and bets will be deducted from it. Many online casinos offer a variety of **[our guides](/guides/)** for deposits and withdrawals.

2.  **Choose Your Bet Size (Credits per Hand):**
 *   Before each hand, you need to decide how many credits you want to bet. Most video poker machines allow you to bet between 1 and 5 credits.
 *   Each credit has a specific **denomination**, such as $0.25, $0.50, or $1.00. For example, if you choose a $0.25 denomination and bet 5 credits, your total bet for that hand will be $1.25 (5 credits x $0.25/credit).
 *   As you will learn, betting the maximum of 5 credits is almost always the mathematically optimal strategy.

3.  **Deal Your First Hand:**
 *   Once your bet is set, press the "Deal" or "Draw" button. The machine will then display five cards face up on the screen. These are your initial hand.

4.  **Decide Which Cards to Hold:**
 *   This is where your skill comes into play. You must decide which of the five cards you want to keep (**hold**) and which you want to discard. You can hold anywhere from zero to all five cards.
 *   To hold a card, simply click or tap on it. The word "HELD" or "HOLD" will usually appear on the card. If you change your mind, click it again to un-hold.

5.  **Draw New Cards:**
 *   After you have selected all the cards you wish to hold, press the "Draw" button again. The cards you did not hold will be replaced with new cards from the virtual deck.

6.  **Evaluate Your Final Hand and Payout:**
 *   The machine will then evaluate your final five-card hand based on standard poker hand rankings (e.g., Pair, Two Pair, Three of a Kind, Straight, Flush, Full House, Four of a Kind, Straight Flush, Royal Flush).
 *   If your hand qualifies for a payout according to the game's paytable, your winnings will be added to your credit balance. If not, your bet is lost, and you can start a new hand.

Each hand is an independent event, determined by an **RNG (Random Number Generator)**. This means the cards you receive are entirely random and fair, dispelling common myths like "the machine knows what you discard." The virtual deck is effectively "shuffled" continuously until you hit the "Draw" button.

## Decoding the Paytable: 9/6 vs. 8/5 Explained

The paytable is the most crucial piece of information on any video poker machine. It tells you exactly how much each winning hand pays and, by extension, indicates the **Return to Player (RTP)** percentage of that specific machine. Understanding paytables is key to finding the most generous games, often referred to as "full pay" machines.

Let us focus on **Jacks or Better**, the most common and beginner-friendly video poker variant. Its name comes from the fact that a pair of Jacks is the lowest hand that pays out.

A Jacks or Better paytable is often described using two numbers, like "9/6" or "8/5." These numbers refer to the payout for a **Full House** and a **Flush**, respectively, when betting 1 credit.

*   **9/6 Jacks or Better:** This means a Full House pays 9 credits, and a Flush pays 6 credits. This is considered a "full pay" machine.
*   **8/5 Jacks or Better:** This means a Full House pays 8 credits, and a Flush pays 5 credits. This is a "short pay" machine.

Let us compare the typical paytables for a 1-credit bet:

| Hand | 9/6 Jacks or Better (Full Pay) | 8/5 Jacks or Better (Short Pay) |
| :-------------- | :----------------------------- | :------------------------------ |
| Royal Flush | 250 | 250 |
| Straight Flush  | 50 | 50 |
| Four of a Kind  | 25 | 25 |
| **Full House**  | **9** | **8** |
| **Flush** | **6** | **5** |
| Straight | 4 | 4 |
| Three of a Kind | 3 | 3 |
| Two Pair | 2 | 2 |
| Jacks or Better | 1 | 1 |

The seemingly small difference in payouts for the Full House and Flush has a significant impact on the game's overall RTP:

*   **9/6 Jacks or Better:** With optimal strategy, this machine offers an RTP of **99.54%**. This means, on average, for every $100 you wager over the long term, you can expect to get back $99.54.
*   **8/5 Jacks or Better:** With optimal strategy, this machine offers an RTP of approximately **97.30%**.

As you can see, the difference of one credit on a Full House and one credit on a Flush translates to a more than 2% difference in RTP. Over thousands of hands, this adds up significantly. This is why experienced players always advise looking for 9/6 machines. You might encounter even worse paytables, such as 7/5 or 6/5, especially in bars or lower-tier gaming venues. As some players on Reddit's r/VideoPoker noted, "trash paytables at bars" are common, offering lower payouts for Full House and Flush compared to machines found on a main casino floor or at reputable online casinos.

> **Tip:** Always check the paytable before you start playing. The payouts for Full House and Flush are typically the first numbers to look at to determine if it is a full-pay or short-pay machine.

## The Golden Rule: Why You Should Always Bet Max Credits

This is perhaps the most critical piece of advice for any video poker player. You should always bet the maximum number of credits, which is typically five. The reason for this "golden rule" lies in the disproportionately higher payout for the **Royal Flush** when you bet five credits compared to any other bet size.

Let us revisit the 9/6 Jacks or Better paytable, comparing a 1-credit bet to a 5-credit bet:

| Hand | Payout (1-Credit Bet) | Payout (5-Credit Bet) |
| :-------------- | :-------------------- | :-------------------- |
| **Royal Flush** | **250** | **4,000** |
| Straight Flush  | 50 | 250 |
| Four of a Kind  | 25 | 125 |
| Full House | 9 | 45 |
| Flush | 6 | 30 |
| Straight | 4 | 20 |
| Three of a Kind | 3 | 15 |
| Two Pair | 2 | 10 |
| Jacks or Better | 1 | 5 |

Notice the Royal Flush payout. For a 1-credit bet, it pays 250 credits. For a 5-credit bet, it pays a massive 4,000 credits.
If the payout scaled linearly, a 5-credit bet should pay 5 x 250 = 1,250 credits for a Royal Flush. Instead, it pays 4,000 credits. This is a bonus of 2,750 credits! This bonus payout is designed to incentivize players to bet max credits.

**How this impacts RTP:**
The Royal Flush is a rare hand, but its huge payout when betting max credits significantly boosts the game's overall RTP. If you play a 9/6 Jacks or Better machine with optimal strategy and bet 1 credit, the RTP drops from 99.54% to approximately 98.25%. That is a substantial difference of over 1.25%, all because you are missing out on the Royal Flush bonus.

For example, if you are playing a $0.25 denomination machine:
*   Betting 1 credit costs $0.25 per hand. A Royal Flush pays $62.50 (250 credits x $0.25).
*   Betting 5 credits costs $1.25 per hand. A Royal Flush pays $1,000 (4,000 credits x $0.25).

While betting 5 credits costs more per hand, it is the only way to access the game's full potential RTP. This is why experts like Bob Dancer and many players on r/VideoPoker consistently advise to "Always Max Bet (5 coins) to unlock the 800x Royal Flush bonus." If your bankroll does not allow for 5-credit bets at a certain denomination, it is better to play a lower denomination machine (e.g., $0.05 or $0.10) at 5 credits, rather than playing a higher denomination at 1 or 2 credits. Some players note that online casinos like FanDuel and DraftKings often offer full-pay paytables even at $0.05 denominations, making it accessible to play max credits.

## Basic Strategy: Which Cards Should You Hold?

Unlike slot machines, video poker requires you to make decisions that impact your chances of winning. While optimal strategy charts can be complex, understanding basic strategy for Jacks or Better can significantly improve your play and bring you close to the theoretical RTP. The goal is to maximize the expected value of your hand.

Here is a simplified strategy guide for Jacks or Better, prioritizing hands from best to worst. You should always hold the highest-ranking hand you can form from your initial five cards.

**Always Hold (in order of priority):**

1.  **Royal Flush:** (e.g., A♠ K♠ Q♠ J♠ T♠) - You cannot improve on this.
2.  **Straight Flush:** (e.g., 9♥ 8♥ 7♥ 6♥ 5♥) - Again, perfect.
3.  **Four of a Kind:** (e.g., 8♣ 8♦ 8♥ 8♠ K♣) - You have already hit the big one.
4.  **Four Cards to a Royal Flush:** (e.g., A♦ K♦ Q♦ J♦ 5♠) - Discard the 5♠ to go for the Royal. This is a crucial hold due to the massive Royal Flush payout.
5.  **Full House:** (e.g., Q♣ Q♦ Q♥ 7♠ 7♣) - Already a strong hand.
6.  **Three of a Kind:** (e.g., 4♠ 4♥ 4♦ K♣ 2♥) - Discard the K♣ and 2♥, hoping for a Four of a Kind or Full House.
7.  **Straight:** (e.g., 2♣ 3♦ 4♥ 5♠ 6♣) - Strong, but not as high as a Full House.
8.  **Flush:** (e.g., A♠ J♠ 8♠ 4♠ 2♠) - Solid hand, but less frequent than a Straight.
9.  **Four Cards to a Straight Flush:** (e.g., J♥ T♥ 9♥ 8♥ 2♦) - Discard the 2♦.
10. **Two Pair:** (e.g., A♠ A♦ 7♥ 7♣ Q♠) - Hold both pairs; discard the Q♠.
11. **High Pair (Jacks or Better):** (e.g., K♣ K♦ 7♠ 4♥ 2♣) - This is your minimum paying hand. Hold the pair, discard the rest. Do NOT break a high pair to chase a straight or flush, as this is a common mistake that significantly lowers your RTP.
12. **Three Cards to a Royal Flush:** (e.g., K♠ Q♠ J♠ 7♦ 2♣) - Discard the 7♦ and 2♣.
13. **Four Cards to a Flush:** (e.g., A♥ J♥ 8♥ 2♥ K♠) - Discard the K♠.
14. **Four Cards to an Outside Straight:** (e.g., 7♣ 8♦ 9♥ T♠ 2♣) - This means you need a card on either end (6 or J) to complete the straight. Discard the 2♣.
15. **Low Pair (Tens or lower):** (e.g., 5♠ 5♦ K♥ 9♣ 3♠) - Hold the pair, discard the K♥, 9♣, and 3♠. Do not hold a "kicker" card as you might in Texas Hold'em; video poker only cares about the best five cards.
16. **Three Cards to a Straight Flush:** (e.g., 7♦ 8♦ 9♦ A♠ K♣) - Discard A♠ K♣.
17. **Any Two Suited High Cards (J, Q, K, A):** (e.g., K♣ Q♣ 7♦ 4♠ 2♥) - Hold K♣ Q♣.
18. **Any One High Card:** (e.g., A♥ 7♠ 4♦ 3♣ 2♠) - Hold the A♥.
19. **Nothing:** If you have none of the above, discard all five cards and draw a new hand.

This simplified list covers most common scenarios. For advanced play, you would consult a full strategy chart, but this gives you a strong foundation. The fear of making a "wrong" move is natural, but by following these guidelines, you will be making mathematically sound decisions most of the time.

## Popular Variants: From Deuces Wild to Bonus Poker

While Jacks or Better is the perfect starting point, video poker offers a variety of exciting variants with different rules and paytables. Each variant requires a slightly different strategy to achieve optimal RTP.

### Jacks or Better (JoB)

*   **Key Feature:** The standard, most common game. Pays for a pair of Jacks or better.
*   **Best Paytable:** 9/6 (Full House pays 9, Flush pays 6). RTP 99.54% with optimal strategy.
*   **Why it is good for beginners:** Simple strategy, low variance, and the highest-paying hands are easy to identify.

### Deuces Wild

*   **Key Feature:** All four 2s (deuces) are wild cards, meaning they can substitute for any other card to form a winning hand. This dramatically increases your chances of hitting strong hands.
*   **Best Paytable:** Often called "Full Pay Deuces Wild" or "Not So Ugly Ducks (NSUD)" for its specific payouts. A common full-pay version pays 5 credits for Four of a Kind (with no deuces), 3 for a Straight, and 2 for Three of a Kind.
*   **RTP:** Full Pay Deuces Wild offers an impressive **99.73% RTP** with optimal strategy, one of the highest in the casino.
*   **Strategy Difference:** Because deuces are wild, you will hold them almost always. Hands like Five of a Kind and Wild Royal Flushes become possible. The minimum qualifying hand is typically Three of a Kind.

### Bonus Poker

*   **Key Feature:** Similar to Jacks or Better, but offers higher payouts for specific Four of a Kind hands, usually for 2s, 3s, 4s, and Aces.
*   **Variations:** You will find "Bonus Poker Deluxe," "Double Bonus Poker," and "Double Double Bonus Poker," each with increasingly volatile paytables that offer even higher payouts for certain Four of a Kind combinations, often at the cost of lower payouts for Two Pair or other common hands.
*   **RTP:** Varies widely, but full-pay versions can reach 99%+ with optimal strategy. For example, full-pay Double Bonus Poker can reach 99.11%.
*   **Strategy Difference:** The enhanced payouts for Four of a Kind hands mean your strategy will prioritize chasing these hands more aggressively than in Jacks or Better. This can lead to higher variance, meaning bigger wins but also longer losing streaks.

When exploring these variants, always remember to check the paytable first. The "full pay" versions are always the best choice for maximizing your long-term returns.
