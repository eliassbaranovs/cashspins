# Brazil's Ruling Workers' Party Files Bill to Repeal Online Gambling Legalization

Brazil's Workers' Party (PT) has submitted a bill, PL-1808/2026, to Congress, proposing a full federal ban on online gambling. This legislative action, filed on April 15, 2026, seeks to repeal the existing regulatory framework established by the Bets Law, which took effect on January 1, 2025. The move creates significant uncertainty for the online gambling industry in Brazil, despite President Lula's previous acknowledgment of the need for congressional action on the matter.

## Key Details: Brazil Online Gambling Ban 2026 Bill Introduced

Deputy Pedro Uczai submitted Bill PL-1808/2026, which has the backing of 68 Workers' Party lawmakers. The bill proposes a comprehensive federal ban on online gambling across Brazil. Its primary objective involves repealing all laws governing online betting previously introduced under the Bets Law.

The proposed ban would encompass all operational aspects of an online gambling platform. This includes advertising, sponsorships, and payment processing. Penalties for non-compliance could be severe. Fines could reach up to two billion Brazilian reais, approximately $385 million, alongside potential prison sentences. An analysis from a compliance expert with an M.Sc. in Financial Regulation would highlight that such comprehensive prohibitions and severe penalties signify a legislative intent to fully dismantle the industry, not merely regulate it. Platforms with over one million users would specifically face requirements to remove all gambling promotional content.

## Context: The Bets Law and Brazil's Gambling Market

Brazil's Bets Law became effective on January 1, 2025, establishing a regulated framework for online gambling. This legislative foundation followed earlier statements from President Lula, who recognized the necessity of congressional action to formalize the sector. The Ministry of Finance has actively worked to combat illegal operations under the existing framework. Authorities released a list of 205 authorized sites linked to 93 companies, while approximately 2,000 unauthorized websites are slated for removal by October 2024.

The government has also implemented stricter measures to control the market. A prohibition on credit card use for online betting took effect in October 2024. An inter-ministerial working group formed to address gambling-related issues. The regulated market has shown substantial growth, with Brazilians reportedly spending over $5 billion a month on online gambling during the first quarter of 2025.

## Impact for Players: What a Brazil Online Betting Ban Could Mean

Should Bill PL-1808/2026 pass, online casino players in Brazil would lose access to regulated gambling platforms. The bill explicitly mandates the blocking of financial transactions related to gambling. It also requires the removal of betting applications and websites. Proponents of the ban argue it would protect individuals from social and economic harms, including debt and addiction.

Critics, however, suggest the ban could inadvertently drive players back to the unregulated black market. Consumer protections are non-existent in such environments. The existing regulated framework includes identity verification, age restrictions, and responsible gaming measures. This framework was designed to offer a safer environment for players. ## Industry Reaction and Next Steps for the PT Gambling Bill

The iGaming industry has expressed strong opposition to the proposed ban. The National Association of Games and Lotteries (ANJL), a key industry trade body, labeled the proposal a "great risk." ANJL emphasized that the existing regulatory framework was specifically designed to bring previously unregulated activities into a controlled and safer environment. The Brazilian Institute for Responsible Gaming (IBJR) also warned that a ban would not eliminate demand for gambling. Instead, it would push demand into the informal sector. Such a move would remove crucial protection mechanisms and significant revenue streams intended for public services.

Brazilian football leagues and media groups, increasingly reliant on high-value sponsorship deals from betting companies, are expected to resist the bill. They cite concerns that such restrictions could destabilize their financial models. Operators and leaders within the fixed-odds betting sector have cautioned that a ban could lead to judicial chaos, legal uncertainty, and paradoxically, strengthen organized crime. In December 2025, the Secretariat of Prizes and Betting (SPA) launched a centralized self-exclusion platform. It saw over 217,000 requests in its first 40 days.
