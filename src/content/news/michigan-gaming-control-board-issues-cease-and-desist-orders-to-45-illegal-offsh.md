# MGCB Cease-and-Desist 2026: Michigan Targets 45 Illegal Offshore Operators

The Michigan Gaming Control Board (MGCB) issued cease-and-desist orders to 45 illegal offshore online gambling operators on April 7, 2026, in a significant move to protect Michigan residents and safeguard the state's regulated iGaming market. This **MGCB Cease-and-Desist 2026** action underscores Michigan's firm stance against unregulated gambling.

The enforcement aims to prevent these unlicensed platforms from targeting Michigan residents, redirecting players towards legally sanctioned and safer options. MGCB Executive Director Henry Williams explicitly stated that Michigan will not tolerate illegal operations undermining lawful businesses within its jurisdiction.

## MGCB Cease-and-Desist 2026: Key Details

The orders, issued on April 7, 2026, signal an intensified effort by the MGCB to combat the proliferation of unregulated online gambling. By targeting 45 distinct offshore entities, the MGCB seeks to protect consumers from the inherent risks associated with illegal sites. This action provides a clear message to operators that Michigan's gambling laws will be enforced to maintain market integrity and ensure player safety.

## Context and Background

This enforcement against illegal offshore gambling operators is part of a growing trend across the United States. States with regulated online gambling markets are increasingly cracking down on unlicensed competition to protect their tax revenues and ensure a safe environment for players. For regulated operators, such as BetMGM, Caesars, DraftKings, and FanDuel, this MGCB Cease-and-Desist 2026 initiative helps level the playing field by removing rivals that do not incur the same licensing fees, compliance costs, or tax obligations.

MGCB Executive Director Henry Williams consistently emphasizes the agency's commitment to upholding the integrity of Michigan's regulated gaming environment. This includes ensuring all gambling activities are conducted lawfully and responsibly, a principle central to the state's approach to iGaming. The broader industry has noted increased scrutiny on unlicensed activities, including previous cease-and-desist orders issued to sweepstakes casinos in states like Connecticut, Delaware, Maryland, and Michigan in early 2025. Several other states, including New York, Virginia, and Maine, are also considering or advancing online casino legislation in 2026, driven by the potential for increased tax revenue and player protection.
