# Federal Government Sues States Over Prediction Market Regulation in 2026

The Commodity Futures Trading Commission (CFTC) filed a landmark lawsuit on April 2, 2026, against Connecticut, Arizona, and Illinois, challenging these states' efforts to regulate prediction market operators. This action asserts the federal government's claim of exclusive jurisdiction over prediction markets, arguing they fall under federal derivatives law rather than state gambling statutes.

## Key Details

The CFTC's lawsuit contends that it holds sole regulatory authority over prediction markets, classifying them as derivatives products under federal oversight. This directly opposes actions taken by the defendant states, which have issued cease-and-desist orders to prediction market operators, viewing their offerings as illegal gambling. Arizona has escalated its enforcement by filing criminal charges against Kalshi, a prominent prediction market platform, for alleged violations of state law. This legal confrontation is poised to significantly influence the operational framework and legal standing of prediction markets across the United States.

## Context & Background

This dispute is part of a broader, ongoing conflict regarding the classification and regulation of prediction markets. CFTC Chairman Michael Selig has publicly stated the agency's commitment to defending its exclusive regulatory authority and protecting market participants from what he describes as "overzealous state regulators." Selig emphasizes the supremacy of federal derivatives laws over state gambling regulations.

Conversely, Connecticut Attorney General William Tong has accused the CFTC of "recycling industry arguments" and has pledged to vigorously defend Connecticut's consumer protection laws. State regulators argue that prediction markets divert tax revenue from licensed sportsbooks and circumvent essential consumer protection measures inherent in state-regulated gambling frameworks.

Prediction market operators, including Kalshi and Polymarket, consistently maintain that their offerings are derivatives products regulated by the Commodity Exchange Act, not gambling services. Kalshi previously initiated federal lawsuits challenging cease-and-desist orders from Nevada and New Jersey. A significant development occurred when the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit upheld a preliminary injunction, preventing New Jersey gaming regulators from enforcing state gambling laws against Kalshi. This ruling was based on the premise that Kalshi's contracts are "swaps" under CFTC jurisdiction. However, a federal court in Nevada had previously ruled in November that sports event contracts constitute gambling, highlighting the mixed legal landscape. The CFTC has also shown support for prediction market operators, filing an amicus brief in favor of Crypto.com in Nevada.

Beyond the states directly involved in the current lawsuit, others including Ohio, Tennessee, and New York have also issued cease-and-desist letters to various prediction market platforms, underscoring the widespread nature of this jurisdictional challenge. The CFTC, in conjunction with the SEC, has released final interpretive guidance for classifying tokenized assets and is actively seeking public comment on an Advanced Notice of Proposed Rulemaking (ANPRM) to potentially amend or introduce new regulations for prediction market event contracts.
