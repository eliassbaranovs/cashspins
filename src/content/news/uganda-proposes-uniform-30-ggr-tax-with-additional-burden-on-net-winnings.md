# Uganda GGR Tax Proposal 2026: New 30% Rate and Player Withholding Tax Proposed

Uganda's government is proposing a new uniform 30% Gross Gaming Revenue (GGR) tax for online gambling operators, alongside an additional 15% withholding tax on players' net winnings, according to legislative proposals tabled in early April 2026. This significant fiscal adjustment, if passed, is slated to take effect on July 1, 2026, and aims to substantially increase government revenue from the gambling sector.

## Key Details of the Uganda GGR Tax Proposal 2026

The core of the proposal involves two distinct tax increases. First, online gambling operators would face a standardized 30% GGR tax. This rate would apply across the board, replacing any existing varied tax structures. Second, a new 15% withholding tax on players' net winnings is under consideration. This tax would be directly deducted by operators before any winnings are disbursed to players, reducing the overall payout received by successful bettors. The Ministry of Finance, led by Finance Minister Matia Kasaija and Minister of State Henry Musasizi, has put forward these measures.

## Context and Industry Concerns

This **Uganda gambling tax** initiative reflects a growing trend of stricter fiscal policies in emerging markets, particularly within the East African region. The government's objective is to enhance its revenue streams from the expanding gambling sector. However, the proposed tax increases have elicited concerns from industry stakeholders. Operators fear that a 30% GGR tax rate could severely squeeze already thin profit margins, potentially leading to smaller firms exiting the market. Industry players also argue that such heavy taxation, especially when combined with the player-facing levy, might inadvertently push consumers towards unregulated offshore platforms, thereby undermining the government's revenue goals and regulatory oversight. The combined 30% GGR tax and 15% player withholding tax would position Uganda among the African continent's highest-taxed gambling markets, impacting the broader **GGR tax Africa** landscape.
